Company Overview
The Origin

Whitney Wolfe Herd cofounded Tinder in 2012. Two years later, she left after a sexual harassment dispute that became public. That experience shaped everything about what she built next.

Bumble launched in 2014 with one rule: women message first. The match expires in 24 hours if they don't. It was a direct response to what Wolfe Herd had watched go wrong at Tinder, and it worked.

In February 2021, Bumble went public. Wolfe Herd became the youngest woman to take a company public in US history. The stock opened at $43, hit $84 within a week, and has since fallen to trade between $3.55 and $9.22.

Market Overview & Competition
The Market
  • Global dating app revenue crossed $6 billion in 2025, then slumped for the first time.
  • 350 million people use dating apps worldwide.
  • Only 22.75 million of them pay for anything (6.5% conversion rate).
  • That means 93.5% of users generate zero direct revenue.
  • The category isn't growing anymore. It's consolidating and contracting.
The Signal
350M users, $6B in revenue, and still declining. People try these apps. They just don't stay.

Business Model

Freemium. Free to download, revenue from premium features. Total revenue in 2025: ~$1.03 billion. Of 350 million global users, 22.75 million pay. That's 6.5%. The other 93.5% generate nothing.

Every premium feature helps you get more matches. None help you get a date. Bumble monetises the search, not the outcome. The moment you succeed, you stop paying.

The Beehive
Bumble's Scoring System

Bumble's matching algorithm is called the Beehive, an ELO style scoring system borrowed from chess. Every user gets an invisible score. That score determines who sees your profile, how often, and in what position. You never see it. You just feel it.

Profile Quality
Photo quality and count40%
Profile completeness30%
Bio quality30%

Activity Score
Login frequency (last 7 days)40%

How often you open the app · how consistently you reply · how actively you swipe.
Interaction Modifiers
Swipe rate 20 to 40%
+1.2x bonus. Treated as selective, high quality.
Swipe rate above 70%
0.5x penalty. Flagged as bot like.
Match with a high score user
Your score increases (ELO mechanic).
First message sent under 6hrs
+1.5x boost.
Active in last 24hrs
+1.5x applied across your entire score.
New user (Day 1 to 7)
Up to 2x temporary visibility boost.
Three Biases Baked In
  1. Looks first. 40% of your score is photos before anyone reads a word you wrote.
  2. Addicts win. Daily logins beat serious daters who check in weekly.
  3. Rich get richer. More matches means higher score means more matches.

What Worked Well
01
A Dedicated Space for Connection
Bumble created a focused environment built only for meeting people. No dual purpose, no distractions. Users show up with intent, which raises the baseline quality of interaction compared to sliding into someone's Instagram DMs.
02
Discovery at Your Fingertips
Finding potential matches is genuinely effortless. Location filters, age range, and intent settings mean relevant profiles surface fast. The friction that made earlier dating methods feel exhausting is gone.
03
Rich Profile Expression
Beyond photos, Bumble gives users real tools to show personality. Written prompts, voice notes, Spotify integration, interest badges. When used well, you get a real sense of someone before the first message is sent.
04
Women Message First, A Real Safety Innovation
The 24hr female first rule was a genuine insight in 2014. It reduced unsolicited messages, gave women control over who enters their inbox, and made the app feel meaningfully safer than alternatives. Bold for its time, and it worked.
05
Scale Creates Possibility
350 million global users. In dense cities, the pool is large enough that the app can deliver real value through proximity and volume alone. Almost anyone can find someone compatible, at least on paper.

08
Where Bumble Falls Short
01
Looks Are Everything
40% of your Beehive score is photos. Before anyone reads your bio, hears your voice note, or sees what you wrote in your prompts, the algorithm has already ranked you on your face. Compatibility, values, and personality are permanently secondary to appearance.
02
High Beehive Score Does Not Mean More Matches
The algorithm can score you well and still not show you to the right people. Magesh has a complete profile, logs in daily, and bought Premium. He gets one match a week. Score measures effort. It does not guarantee outcome.
03
Profile Effort Goes Unrewarded
Users who fill every prompt, record voice notes, and write thoughtful bios are not meaningfully rewarded in visibility over someone with three good photos and nothing else. Ayush barely filled his profile. He gets 15+ matches a week. The signal the algorithm trusts most is still the face.
04
The More You Swipe, The Emptier It Feels
Swiping is a Skinner box. After 100 swipes, profiles stop feeling like people and start feeling like cards. Users report burnout not because they didn't find anyone, but because the mechanic itself erodes what connection is supposed to feel like.
05
Built for Engagement, Not Dates
Daily login bonuses, streak rewards, match expiry pressure, Spotlight boosts. Every gamification layer maximises time on app. None of it is designed to get you on an actual date. Bumble measures DAUs and subscription revenue. It does not measure dates had, relationships formed, or users who left because they found someone.

What a Better Dating App Would Do Differently
01
Weight Personality, Not Just Photos
Reduce photo weight in scoring from 40% to 20%. Compensate with prompt quality, response thoughtfulness, and conversation depth. Compatibility filters (values, lifestyle, intent) carry real ranking weight.
Trade offAttractive users drive aspirational downloads. Reducing their advantage risks reducing top of funnel appeal and the aspirational effect that keeps mid tier users paying for Premium.
02
Decouple Beehive Score from Match Visibility
A complete, thoughtful profile in Coimbatore should get proportionally more visibility within its local pool, not get buried because the pool is small. Effort and geography shouldn't compound each other.
Trade offThe ELO system creates addictive aspiration. Everyone wants to climb the invisible ladder. Flattening it reduces the anxiety that drives Spotlight and Boost purchases.
03
Also Reward Profile Depth Over Photo Quality
Surface voice notes, prompts, and shared interests as primary match signals, not afterthoughts. Users like Magesh who invest in their profile should demonstrably out match users like Ayush who don't.
Trade offEffort rewarding systems are slower to show results. Users expect instant gratification. A depth first approach increases early drop off before the payoff arrives.
04
Replace the Swipe with Intentional Discovery
Limit daily swipes. Not to gatekeep, but to force intentionality. Fewer swipes per day means each one carries more weight. Profiles stop feeling like cards and start feeling like people.
Trade offSwipe volume is a core engagement metric. Reducing it lowers session time, which hurts advertising revenue and app store ranking signals.
05
Measure Dates, Not DAUs
The north star metric shifts to dates had, not daily active users. Ask users post match: did you meet? Track the funnel all the way to the real world, not just to the inbox.
Trade offIf Bumble optimises for dates, users delete the app faster. Every successful date is a lost subscriber. Fixing the metric means accepting that success looks like churn, and building a business model that survives that.
06
Close the Loop. The 'We Met' Feature
After a conversation crosses a depth signal (messages exchanged, time spent), Bumble surfaces one button in the chat: 'Did you meet up?' with Yes or Not yet options. If yes, both users get a short post date card.

The Post Date Card

  • Did they show up as their profile suggested?
  • Did you feel safe?
  • Would you recommend them to a friend?

What This Data Does

  • Feeds real world behaviour into the Beehive score for the first time.
  • Users who show up well on dates get boosted visibility.
  • Users who misrepresent themselves get quietly deprioritised.
  • Bumble finally has a metric connecting in app behaviour to real outcomes.

Trade Off

  • Makes the app measurably better at getting people off it.
  • Opens a trust and safety liability around disputed ratings.
  • But a reputation system creates a moat no competitor has, and users who trust the platform stay longer, refer more, and pay more willingly.

How to Read This
Q1Quick wins
Quick wins that build user trust without touching the core algorithm. These send a signal to users that Bumble is serious about real outcomes.
Q2Structural fixes
Structural fixes requiring careful A/B testing. Algorithm changes need controlled rollout to avoid unintended drops in engagement.
Q3Deep system rewrites
Decoupling Beehive score and rewarding profile depth means rebuilding matching logic at its core. High effort, but this is where the product starts feeling fundamentally different.
Q4The hardest conversation
Shifting north star from DAUs to dates is a board level decision, not a product decision. Everything in Q1 through Q3 builds the business case for why it's necessary.
Bumble has to decide whether it wants to be the app that keeps you swiping or the app that actually works. Right now, it can't be both.
